॥ शिवोऽहम् ॥

Compiled, in seva, over six years.

The questions in this archive were addressed to Guruji over the course of six years, in private correspondence and on the small Telegram sangha he kept. They were not written for publication; what is gathered here are the answers worth keeping — the ones his students returned to, more than once, when their own practice grew thin.

The Sanskrit is set in देवनागरी alongside transliteration, so a reader unfamiliar with the script may still hear the syllables as they are meant to sound. Where Guruji used a phrase whose ordinary translation would dilute it, the original has been left and a note placed beside.

"The questions are not mine. The answers are not mine. What is mine is the silence between them — and even that, I borrowed."

The codex is offered freely, in the manner of anugraha — grace passed along, not held. New entries are added as they arise. Older entries are revised, gently, when a wiser reading appears.

ॐ — compiled by the students of the Sangha, 2026.

    BHAIRAVA ANUGRAHA / FOLIO I · MANTRA & JAPA / ENTRY XLII

    On missing the count,
    and the absence of grief.

    14 March 2024 № 173
    QUESTION

    "Guruji, namaskāra. I sat for japa last night, the Baṭuka mantra, mālā in hand, and somewhere past sixty I lost the count. I was thinking about office, then about a friend who is unwell. When I returned to the breath I was already on the eighty-something bead but I am not sure."

    "Should I begin the round again from one, or carry on the count from where I think I was, or finish the mālā and add a few more for safety? I am sorry to ask such a small thing. — Vinod"

    Begin again, Vinod. But begin without grief.

    The count is a fence around the practice; it is not the practice itself. If the fence breaks, mend it — do not weep over the field. The bead exists to give the mind a small task, so that the larger one — the inward attention — has room to settle. When the count slips, the mind has not failed; it has merely shown you where its hinges are loose.

    मन एव मन्त्रः, मन एव बन्धः, मन एव मोक्षः — the mind itself is the mantra, the mind itself is the bondage, the mind itself is the liberation. A mind that turns to grief over a miscount has already broken count more thoroughly than any slip of the bead.

    "Sit again. Breathe once. Begin from one. The mantra is patient — it has waited longer than your lineage."

    Practical guidance: when the slip is small (as in your case, perhaps fifteen or twenty beads of inattention), complete the round and add an explicit kṣamā-prārthanā at its close — the short prayer of pardon — and proceed to the next round. When the slip is large, or when the mind has wandered for so long that the count has become guesswork, set the mālā aside, sit empty-handed for three breaths, and start a fresh round from the bead beside the meru.

    Do not add "a few more for safety." Quantity is not the path. The path is the quality of presence in whatever number is offered. Five beads said with the whole self are heavier than ten thousand said with half a mind.

    — Guruji ॐ
